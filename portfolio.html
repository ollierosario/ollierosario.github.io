Nuu-chah-nulth Language Project

ʕiniiƛm̓inḥč̓a t̕aatn̓aʔis, or The Dog Children, is a story told by Caroline Little for her and Toshihide Nakayama's Nuu-chah-nulth language documentation project, Caroline Little's Nuu-chah-nulth (Ahousaht) Texts with Grammatical Analysis. Nuuchahnulth, like all BC First Nations languages, is endangered. The Ahousaht dialect of Nuuchahnulth has, as of 2011, 154 learning speakers. An aspect of the language many learners struggle with is the highly polymorphemic nature of the words. Words are formed using roots and affixes, but they cannot be combined randomly, the language, like any other, follows a grammar. In the case of Nuuchahnulth, this grammar is very different from what English speakers have been exposed to. Due to many destructive effects of colonialism, learners are not often able to learn the language through immersion. Because of this, learners, regardless of age, are very likely to speak English.

  To mediate the difficulty of understanding word structure, I prepared an interactive website presenting the story ʕiniiƛm̓inḥč̓a t̕aatn̓aʔis. On the site, morphemes are highlighted blue or purple depending on whether they are lexical or functional. Lexical morphemes carry meaning, while functional morphemes carry a grammatical purpose. An example of a functional morpheme in English would be -ed for past tense. The example on the left shows how hovering over a highlighted morpheme provides its definition. The other pages on the site have explanations of word construction like this, and comprehension questions designed to help learners build words. On every page, there is a vocabulary list in the sidebar.

The Bells

Programmers: Ollie Rosario, Micki Lee-Smith
Choreographer, Dancer: Gemma Tomasky

The Bells is a piece inspired by Edgar Allan Poe's poem of the same name. Our interpretation of the change in tone through the poem gave our piece a narrative of instability. The character moves through zones that are increasingly chaotic and dangerous, becoming more scared and confused, before finally being caught in the center. The sound samples used in each zone are meant to reflect this, and the themes of loss and madness.

reflections

Reflections, made in the aftermath of hurricane Irma, challenges the viewer to understand the role of colonialism in the environmental disasters plaguing Puerto Rico. The mirrors incorporate the viewer into the work, placing them in the position of an onlooker. The flora and fauna are endemic to the island, and chosen to display the natural diversity. The handmade figures are to scale, the same size as real specimens.

In planning this sculpture, I researched species endemic to Puerto Rico. One of the species depicted is Thespesia grandiflora, known as la flor de maga. It is the national flower of Puerto Rico. While we are not a sovereign people, there is a strong national identity that is often rooted in the natural features of the island. This flower is enormous, typically measuring 13 cm, and, like all the other wire figures in Reflections, has been depicted to scale.

Also close to the identity of the island is the coquí. The word coquí refers to mating call of the males, and encompasses multiple species. Less iconic are bats, the only land mammals native to Puerto Rico. The wing featured is that of a big brown bat, Eptesicus fuscus. Bats are essential to limit the population of mosquitoes on the island.

cesium-137

cesium-137, a woodblock print carved on a Medex block, explores decay in the aftermath of the Chernobyl disaster. I focus on the contexts of ecological and message decay. The nearby city of Pripyat was established expressly to house the workers of the power plant, in an age where the prevailing message was that of the sculpture Peaceful Atom: nuclear power was the future, and it was safe and efficient. In the context of message decay, the response at Pripyat represents a near instantaneous corruption of meaning. The city was rendered a ghost town, and its message instantly became one of disaster rather than prosperity. In my print, this is reflected by the iconic ferris wheel from the never opened Pripyat Amusement Park.

The ecological context of the print surrounds a lack of decay. Within the Chernobyl exclusionzone, litter mass loss is 40% lower than in the rest of Ukraine. This is thought to be a result of the disaster's impact on the populations of decomposers. Within the Red Forest, this is most notable. The trees that died from the radiation levels took on a red tint, depicted in the choice of ink in the print. I used noise to reflect the high levels of deceased organic matter on the floor, which carries a sense of disorder and stasis. There is a nature of slowed time in this space, where nothing grows and nothing decays. Cesium, the most prominent element in the exclusion zone, just reached its first half life in 2016, meaning half of the isotope's radiation released in the disaster has decayed.

storytelling

storytelling is a visual project concerning perceived limitations of print media. Through use of embroidery, watercolor, collage, and more, I create an abstract story. The plot is highly open, rendering the viewer able to interpret the project however they see fit. While still ongoing, the end goal is to put together a small book of these illustrations, with my personal narrative in mind, but without words.

zither sin string

zither sin string, my capstone project for the minor in Applied Music Technology, is an electronic instrument driven by gestures. It uses two ultrasonic distance sensors and an Adafruit APDS9960 breakout board. The breakout board is capable of tracking gestures, color, and distance. Currently, only the gesture tracking is in use, detecting left, right, up, and downward motions. When these are reported to the software, sound is triggered. The different motions control the timbre of sound, changing the constants input to my syntehsizer. The ultrasonic distance sensors, positioned on opposite sides of the instrument, are used to measure how far away the performer's hand is. This distance is then transformed into pitches. The program stores the most recent pitch, and outputs it through the desired synthesis when a gesture is detected.
building the casing
  One of the extended goals of this project is to implement multiple synthesizers and create an "all purpose string instrument." This would use different gestures to switch between bowed string synthesis and plucked string synthesis, and potentially use the distance tracking capabilities of the APDS to trigger hammered string timbres.
Arduino and sensors

  To assemble the hardware, I learned how to solder and desolder. The ultrasonic sensors had to have the pins removed and swapped with new ones so they would point upward. I wired the components to a shield so the components can be easily removed from the Arduino. In designing the casing, I considered multiple options, including making the entire thing out of wood. Aesthetically, I wanted the instrument to appear like a dulcimer, so I decided to make the casing out of a decorative box. I printed my design so i could cut it cleanly, and I'm quite pleased with the result. It is supported with wooden blocks and pegs, two of which keep the electronics, which are attached to a sheet of craft board, in line with the lid.
